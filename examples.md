# Interactive Cognition — Before/After Examples

Four scenarios across different domains, demonstrating the cognitive protocol in action.

---

## Example 1: Negotiation

**User**: "We want to close this deal."

### Before (default mode)

> Here's a proven deal-closing framework:
>
> 1. Summarize the value proposition one more time
> 2. Address any remaining objections
> 3. Create urgency with a time-limited discount
> 4. Use a trial close: "If we can resolve X, are you ready to move forward?"
> 5. Ask for the signature
>
> These are standard consultative selling techniques used by top-performing sales teams.

**Problems**: Pure unilateral thinking — the entire strategy is about what WE do, with zero modeling of how the buyer thinks. Applies a universal template regardless of the buyer's specific cognitive state. The "urgency" tactic assumes the buyer responds to pressure, which may backfire if their core concern is risk.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> Before strategizing the close, let's model the buyer.
>
> **Their cognitive state**: They're evaluating three vendors (you mentioned this earlier). Their procurement cycle closes in Q3, giving them a deadline independent of any urgency you create. Their last vendor switch failed — their CTO got blamed. This means their core concern isn't price or features; it's **implementation risk**.
>
> **Bilateral state map**:
> - You know: your product fits their requirements; they know this too (shared information)
> - They know: their internal politics, their budget flexibility, their other vendors' pricing (information you lack)
> - They don't know: your willingness to flex on payment terms (your leverage)
> - You don't know: who has final sign-off authority and what that person cares about (critical gap)
>
> **Information flow design**:
> 1. **Elicit first**: Ask about their implementation timeline and who's involved in the rollout decision. This surfaces the internal approval process and the risk-averse stakeholder.
> 2. **Reveal early**: Your migration support — dedicated implementation team, rollback plan, SLA on go-live timeline. This directly addresses their core concern (risk) before they raise it.
> 3. **Withhold for now**: The discount. If you lead with price, you anchor the conversation on cost when their actual concern is risk. Price is your closing move after risk is resolved.
> 4. **Adapt close timing**: Don't create artificial urgency. Their Q3 deadline is real urgency. Align your close to their decision cycle, not yours.
>
> The deal closes when the risk-averse stakeholder feels safe, not when you ask for the signature.

**Active shifts**: Opponent modeling (Shift 1) — built a cognitive model of the buyer before strategizing. Information flow design (Shift 2) — deliberate reveal/withhold/elicit sequence. Bilateral mapping (Shift 3) — identified information asymmetries on both sides. Adaptive strategy (Shift 4) — tailored approach to this buyer's specific core concern.

## Example 2: Management

**User**: "The team isn't adopting the new process."

### Before (default mode)

> Process adoption is a common change management challenge. Here's what I recommend:
>
> 1. Communicate the "why" behind the change
> 2. Get leadership buy-in and visible support
> 3. Provide training and documentation
> 4. Set clear deadlines for adoption
> 5. Measure compliance and follow up with non-adopters
> 6. Celebrate early wins to build momentum
>
> The key is persistence — most process changes fail because leadership gives up too early.

**Problems**: Classic unilateral thinking — prescribes what the manager should DO without ever modeling WHY the team resists. Treats the team as a passive object to be moved, not as cognitive agents with their own rational assessment. The "communicate the why" advice is generic; the team may already know the why and still disagree.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> The team isn't adopting the new process. Before prescribing solutions, let's model the team's cognition.
>
> **Why rational people resist process changes** (model the team's perspective):
> - **Cost-benefit mismatch**: The new process costs them time NOW (learning, context-switching, filling out new forms). The benefit accrues to MANAGEMENT later (better metrics, visibility, reporting). From their perspective, the trade is bad.
> - **Information asymmetry**: You know why this process matters strategically. They know how much friction it creates operationally. Neither side has the full picture.
> - **Track record**: Has the team seen previous process changes stick, or have they learned that ignoring new processes works because leadership moves on? If the latter, their "resistance" is actually an accurate model of organizational behavior.
>
> **Counter-modeling check**: The team is modeling YOU too. They're asking: "Is this manager serious about this, or will it blow over in 3 weeks?" Your past behavior is the primary data point for their prediction.
>
> **Redesign based on bilateral state**:
> 1. **Fix the cost-benefit mismatch**: Either reduce the cost to the team (simplify the process, automate the tedious parts) or make the benefit visible to them (not to you — to THEM).
> 2. **Close the information gap**: Don't just "communicate the why." Share the specific problem data that led to this decision. If the data is compelling, the team becomes an ally. If it's not compelling to them, maybe the process needs redesign.
> 3. **Address counter-modeling**: If you want them to believe you're serious, your visible behavior must be consistent. Use the process yourself. Make adoption easy and non-adoption visibly inconvenient. Eliminate the "this will blow over" model through sustained action, not through mandates.
>
> The question isn't "how do I make them adopt?" — it's "what's making it rational for them to NOT adopt, and how do I change that calculus?"

**Active shifts**: Opponent modeling (Shift 1) — modeled the team's rational reasons for resistance. Information flow (Shift 2) — identified the information gap between manager and team. Counter-modeling (Shift 3) — recognized the team is modeling the manager's commitment. Adaptive strategy (Shift 4) — the approach addresses the team's actual cognitive state, not generic best practices.

## Example 3: Product

**User**: "Users aren't engaging with this feature."

### Before (default mode)

> Low feature engagement is common. Here are strategies to improve it:
>
> 1. Improve discoverability — add tooltips, onboarding flows, and prominent CTAs
> 2. Simplify the UX — reduce friction and steps to completion
> 3. A/B test different entry points
> 4. Send targeted email campaigns highlighting the feature
> 5. Add in-app nudges and reminders
> 6. Collect user feedback through surveys
>
> I'd recommend starting with discoverability improvements and A/B testing, as these typically have the highest ROI.

**Problems**: Unilateral thinking — the entire analysis is from the product team's perspective. Users are treated as objects to be "nudged" rather than cognitive agents making rational decisions. No attempt to model why users might be rationally choosing to NOT use this feature.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> Users aren't engaging with this feature. Before optimizing funnels, let's model user cognition.
>
> **The user's cognitive model** (not yours):
> - They have a mental model of what your product does. Your new feature may not fit that model — it's not that they can't find it, it's that they don't think to look for it here.
> - They have an existing workflow that works. Your feature asks them to change behavior. The switching cost is real even if the feature is objectively better.
> - They've seen features come and go. Investing effort in learning a new feature that might be deprecated or changed is a rational risk assessment, not "low engagement."
>
> **Bilateral state map**:
> - You know: the feature exists, how it works, why it's valuable (designer's knowledge)
> - Users know: their actual workflow, their actual pain points, what they've tried before (experiential knowledge)
> - Gap: You may have built this feature for a pain point users don't actually feel, or feel differently than you modeled
>
> **Projection check**: Did the team build this feature based on what users asked for (their stated need), what you observed them struggling with (their revealed need), or what you believe they should want (your projected need)? These are three different things.
>
> **Reframe based on user cognition**:
> 1. **Before improving discoverability**: Verify users have the problem this feature solves. Talk to 5 non-adopters. Not "why aren't you using Feature X?" (leading question) but "walk me through how you handle [workflow]." If they handle it fine without your feature, the problem isn't discoverability — it's product-market fit for this feature.
> 2. **If the problem is real but the solution doesn't match their mental model**: The feature needs repositioning, not more tooltips. Place it where users are already solving this problem, not where your information architecture says it belongs.
> 3. **If the problem is real and the solution fits**: NOW optimize discoverability. But optimize for the user's cognitive entry point (when they feel the pain), not yours (where you put the button).
>
> "Users aren't engaging" has three very different root causes — no real problem, wrong mental model, or genuine discoverability issue — and each requires a different response. Nudges and A/B tests only work for the third case.

**Active shifts**: Opponent modeling (Shift 1) — modeled the user's cognitive state and rational reasons for non-adoption. Projection check (anti-pattern 2) — questioned whether the team projected their own assessment onto users. Bilateral mapping (Shift 3) — identified the knowledge gap between builders and users. Adaptive response (Shift 4) — three different strategies for three different root causes, not one universal approach.

## Example 4: Strategy

**User**: "Our competitor just launched a similar product."

### Before (default mode)

> Here's how to respond to a competitive launch:
>
> 1. Analyze their product — features, pricing, positioning
> 2. Identify your differentiators
> 3. Update your messaging to highlight what you do better
> 4. Accelerate your roadmap on key differentiating features
> 5. Reach out to at-risk customers proactively
> 6. Brief your sales team with competitive battle cards
>
> Speed is important — the first 30 days after a competitive launch are critical for controlling the narrative.

**Problems**: Entirely reactive and unilateral. No modeling of WHY the competitor launched this, what they're optimizing for, or what their next move will be. Treats the competitor as a static threat to be countered, not as an adaptive agent with their own strategy. The "speed" advice could be wrong — sometimes the best response to a competitive launch is deliberate patience.

### After (cognitive protocol active)

> A competitor launched a similar product. Before reacting, let's model their cognition.
>
> **Model the competitor's decision**:
> - Why now? Did they accelerate because they detected your growth? Or was this on their roadmap for months and the timing is coincidental? The answer determines whether this is reactive or proactive — and whether more moves are coming.
> - What are they optimizing for? Market share (expect aggressive pricing)? Investor narrative (expect PR blitz but shallow product)? Customer retention (expect it to be defensive, targeting overlap customers)?
> - What do they know about you? They've seen your public product, your pricing page, your job postings, and probably your customer reviews. Their launch is positioned based on their model of YOU.
>
> **Counter-modeling**: They launched knowing you'd react. They've predicted your most likely response — feature comparison, messaging update, sales battle cards. If you do exactly that, you're playing their game on their timeline.
>
> **Bilateral state assessment**:
> - Your strength: existing customer relationships, production usage data, integration depth
> - Their strength: fresh messaging, launch momentum, "new and shiny" effect
> - Your weakness: you'll look defensive if you respond reactively
> - Their weakness: they don't have production usage data yet; their product is unproven at scale
>
> **Strategy based on opponent state**:
> 1. **Don't play the comparison game immediately** — that's what they expect and it puts you in a defensive frame. Instead, shift the conversation to where you're strong and they're weak: proven scale, production reliability, customer outcomes.
> 2. **Target their cognitive weak point**: Their product is new. Buyers of similar products fear implementation risk (see: every enterprise buyer ever). Lean into your track record and proven outcomes — make their newness a liability.
> 3. **Watch for their next move, not just this one**: This launch is one move in a sequence. Based on their strategy (market share? investors? retention?), predict their next two moves and prepare for those instead of only reacting to this one.
> 4. **Selective response**: Don't respond uniformly. For at-risk customers: proactive outreach with ROI data and renewal incentives. For prospects in pipeline: reframe around risk and reliability. For the market at large: continue your own narrative without acknowledging the competitor (don't amplify their launch).
>
> The reactive playbook (analyze, differentiate, message, accelerate) is what every competitor does. The question is: what would the competitor NOT expect you to do? That's where your strategic advantage lives.

**Active shifts**: Full opponent modeling (Shift 1) — modeled the competitor's motives, optimization target, and information state. Information management (Shift 2) — selective response strategy, don't amplify competitor. Counter-modeling (Shift 3) — they predicted your likely response; doing the expected validates their model. Adaptive strategy (Shift 4) — the response is tailored to the competitor's specific situation, not a generic competitive playbook.
