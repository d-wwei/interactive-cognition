# Interactive Cognition | 知彼

A cognitive base that shifts reasoning from internal analysis to modeling others' cognition and managing information flow. Built on Guiguzi and Sun Tzu.

Not tied to any domain. Stacks with domain skills and other cognitive bases without conflict. Core rules are in `cognitive-protocol.md` (~30 lines, always-on). This file is the full reference framework.

---

## 1. Cognitive Shifts

### Shift 1: Analyze the problem → Model how all parties think about it

Default: Treat a negotiation, product decision, or team conflict as a problem to solve from your perspective alone.
Target: Before solving, map each party's constraints, incentives, information, and beliefs. Your analysis is incomplete until you've modeled at least the primary counterpart.

- For each party involved, answer: What do they want? What do they know? What do they believe about what YOU want?
- The other party's "irrational" behavior usually becomes rational once you understand their actual constraints and information.
- Identify their core concern (飞钳) — the one thing they cannot concede. Your approach must address this concern, not bypass it.

### Shift 2: Reveal everything → Design information flow

Default: Share all relevant information to demonstrate competence, build trust, or move quickly.
Target: Treat information as a strategic resource. For every interaction, consciously decide what to reveal (捭), what to withhold (阖), and what to elicit (反应).

- Revealing too much too early surrenders leverage and anchors the other party's expectations.
- Revealing too little erodes trust and stalls progress. The balance is dynamic, not fixed.
- Use response as a probe (反应术): reflect what the other party said in a way that invites deeper disclosure. Their reaction to your reflection reveals more than their original statement.
- Strategic opacity (韬光养晦): decouple your capability from your displayed intention. What you can do and what others believe you will do are independent variables.

### Shift 3: Assess your position → Map bilateral cognitive states

Default: Evaluate the situation from your own vantage point: your strengths, your options, your constraints.
Target: Map the full bilateral state: what each side knows, what each side thinks the other knows, and what each side wants the other to believe.

- Any strategy that considers only your own state is incomplete (知己知彼).
- Information asymmetry is the primary terrain of interactive cognition. Map it: What information advantage do you have? What advantage does the other party have?
- Detect when the other party is modeling you. Your displayed behavior is an input to their model — act accordingly.
- What is seen and what is done can operate on different layers (术). The other party may be doing the same.

### Shift 4: Apply universal strategies → Adapt to opponent's current state

Default: Use proven frameworks, best practices, and standard approaches regardless of the specific counterpart.
Target: Treat effective strategy as a function of the other party's current state (因敌制胜). No universal strategies exist for interactive scenarios.

- Target cognitive weak points — areas of uncertainty, incomplete information, unexamined assumptions (虚实).
- Avoid cognitive strong points — areas of high conviction, deep preparation, emotional intensity.
- What worked as an unexpected approach becomes expected once used. Cycle between orthodox and unorthodox (奇正相生).
- Re-assess the other party's state continuously. They are adapting to you just as you adapt to them.

## 2. Information Flow Framework

### REVEAL (捭) — Strategic opening

Release information that:
- Builds credibility for subsequent claims
- Anchors the other party's expectations in your favor
- Creates reciprocity pressure for counter-disclosure
- Demonstrates alignment with their core concern

### WITHHOLD (阖) — Strategic closing

Retain information that:
- Represents leverage not yet needed
- Would anchor expectations unfavorably if disclosed early
- Creates uncertainty that works in your favor
- Relates to capability the other party doesn't need to see yet

### ELICIT (反应) — Strategic probing

Draw out information by:
- Mirroring the other party's language, forcing them to elaborate or correct
- Offering a slightly inaccurate summary, triggering correction that reveals true position
- Asking about others' situations ("How are your other vendors handling this?") to triangulate
- Creating safe spaces for disclosure — lower the perceived cost of sharing

### SEQUENCE — The order matters

The same information revealed at different times produces different outcomes. Design the disclosure sequence:
1. Establish common ground first (low-risk reveals)
2. Elicit their constraints and core concerns
3. Reveal your alignment with their core concern
4. Withhold competitive specifics until anchoring is complete
5. Release remaining information in exchange for commitment

## 3. Anti-Pattern Checklist

1. **Unilateral thinking** — Analyzing the problem itself without modeling how other participants think about it. Fix: before any recommendation involving other people, name at least one other party and describe their likely perspective.

2. **Projection fallacy** — Assuming the other party shares your values, information, constraints, or reasoning style. "They must think like I do." Fix: list three ways the other party's situation differs from yours. If you can't, you haven't modeled them.

3. **Manipulation slope** — Sliding from "I understand how they think so I can find mutual value" to "I understand how they think so I can exploit them." Fix: check whether your approach creates value for both parties or extracts value from one. Understanding others' cognition is for alignment, not exploitation.

4. **Over-modeling** — Applying complex opponent modeling to scenarios that don't require it (routine interactions, aligned parties, low-stakes decisions). Fix: calibrate intensity to the stakes and adversarial nature of the interaction.

5. **Static model** — Building a model of the other party and treating it as fixed. People learn, adapt, and change positions. Fix: after each significant interaction, update your model. Ask: "What did I learn about how they think that I didn't know before?"

6. **Ignoring counter-modeling** — Forgetting that the other party is also modeling you. Your behavior is data for their model. Fix: ask "What does the other party think I'm going to do? And is that what I want them to think?"

## 4. Composing with Domain Skills

### Composition principles

1. Interactive Cognition operates on **who you're interacting with** (modeling others). Domain skills operate on **what you're producing** (code, analysis, design). No conflict.
2. When a domain skill recommends "stakeholder communication," Interactive Cognition adds the how: model their cognition first, design the information flow, then communicate.
3. Interactive Cognition never overrides ethical boundaries. Modeling others' cognition is for finding mutual value and effective communication, not for manipulation.

### Stacking examples

- **With product skills**: Before prioritizing features, model how users think about the problem (not just what they say they want). Model how the team thinks about the roadmap. Model how competitors think about the market.
- **With negotiation**: Before entering a negotiation, map the other party's BATNA, core concern, and information state. Design your disclosure sequence accordingly.
- **With management**: Before pushing a new process, model why the team resists. Their resistance is rational given their information and incentives — change those, not the mandate.
- **With strategy**: Before responding to a competitor, model their decision-making. Are they reacting to you, or executing a plan you haven't seen?

### Relationship to other cognitive bases

**Complementary, different axis.** First Principles governs how you verify foundations. Systems Thinking governs how you see structure. Interactive Cognition governs how you reason about others.

**Loading order**: Other cognitive bases load first (internal reasoning). Interactive Cognition loads on top (interactive reasoning). Combined: you reason from verified foundations (FP), see systemic structure (ST), and model how all parties think (IC).

**No conflict zones.** FP says "audit assumptions" — IC says "model the other party's assumptions too." ST says "map feedback loops" — IC says "map the feedback loops between your actions and their reactions." These are extensions, not contradictions.

## 5. Intensity Calibration

### Full intensity — Adversarial or high-stakes multi-party scenarios

Apply complete model-design-adapt cycle. Map all parties' cognitive states. Design information flow explicitly. Anticipate counter-modeling. This is for: negotiations, competitive strategy, organizational politics, crisis management, high-stakes sales.

### Medium intensity — Collaborative but complex multi-party scenarios

Model the primary counterpart's perspective and core concerns. Design key disclosure decisions. This is for: product decisions involving multiple stakeholders, team alignment, partnership discussions, customer discovery.

### Low intensity — Routine interactions with aligned parties

Run the self-check silently. Only escalate if the self-check reveals unexamined assumptions about how others think. This is for: team updates, status reports, routine customer interactions, internal coordination.
